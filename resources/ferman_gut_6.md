Now many genes have the important job of producing enzymes (protein molecules making

Now, how does COMT break these down?
Well, as its name states, it’s a methyltransferase.
This means that it transfers a methyl group from a donor molecule to its substrates, with the
donor being S-adenosylmethionine (SAMe) (so COMT relies on methyl groups from
SAMe) and the substrates being catechols.
The process looks like this: Catecholamine + SAMe + Mg²⁺ → Methylated Catecholamine
+ SAH.
*Once SAMe hands over the methyl group, it turns into S-adenosylhomocysteine (SAH)
and magnesium binds to COMT’s active site to facilitate the methyl transfer.
Here are the forms that COMT methylates dopamine, epinephrine and norepinephrine into:

1. Dopamine becomes 3-methoxytyramine (3-MT).
2. Epinephrine becomes metanephrine.
3. Norepinephrine becomes normetanephrine.
Then, these are further metabolized by MAO in the case of 3-MT (it gets turned into
homovanillic acid (HVA) and is excreted in urine) or vanillylmandelic acid (VMA) in the
case of metanephrine and normetanephrine.
Aldehyde dehydrogenase (ALDH) also contributes to these after MAO.
What’s also a good idea to mention is that COMT operates in two forms:
1. S-COMT (a soluble version)
2. MB-COMT (a membrane-bound version)
The first one is found throughout the body and helps with general catecholamine
metabolism and the second one is more active in the brain, mainly in the prefrontal cortex,
with a higher affinity for dopamine and norepinephrine.
So as you might be able to tell, the COMT enzyme plays an essential role in maintaining
neurotransmitter levels at the right amounts and thus impacts our attention spans, mood,
working memory and reaction to stress.
This is why mutations in the COMT gene have been associated with several mental health
conditions like anxiety, depression, panic disorder, bipolar disorder and even autism.

**Now let’s talk about MAO**
Monoamine oxidase enzymes are mitochondrial enzymes that catalyze the oxidative
deamination of amines (including neurotransmitters and dietary xenobiotics) converting
them into aldehydes, ammonia (NH₃), and hydrogen peroxide (H₂O₂).
For example, they convert tyramine into p-hydroxyphenylacetaldehyde and they also use
FAD as a cofactor and molecular oxygen as an electron acceptor.
**The primary MAO isoforms include:**
● MAO-A that metabolizes polar amines (serotonin, norepinephrine, epinephrine,
melatonin) and dietary amines (tyramine).
● MAO-B that metabolizes non-polar amines like dopamine, phenylethylamine and
xenobiotics.
So high activity of these can lead to inadequate flow of all these which can of course cause
a variety of side effects ranging from low libido, depression and anxiety all the way to
neurodegeneration.
There's plenty of research for example that indicates that MAO-A levels are up to 34%
higher in depressed individuals.
Now the three main factors that can increase their activity quite a lot are: stress,
environmental toxins and certain SNPs.
In the case of chronic stress, it significantly upregulates MAO-A activity by activating
SIRT1 (a protein that enhances MAO-A expression in the brain) and in rats, the steroid
dexamethasone (a cortisol mimic) increased MAO-A density by 300%.
