**The system lesson 11: Understanding and improving the function of key hormone receptors.** 

First and foremost, let’s do a brief recap on hormones. 

Hormones are signaling molecules produced by endocrine glands, neuroendocrine cells or other specialized tissues and coordinate a wide range of functions. 

The endocrine system comprises a network of glands and tissues, including: 

**Number 1: The pituitary gland.** 

This small gland (approximately 1 cm in diameter, weighing 0.5–1 gram) is located at the base of the brain, controlled by the hypothalamus, and receives blood via the superior and inferior hypophyseal arteries. 

It has two distinct regions: 

1. Anterior pituitary: Comprises 75–80% of the gland’s mass, composed of glandular epithelial cells organized into cords around blood vessels. 

Cell types include somatotrophs, thyrotrophs, corticotrophs, gonadotrophs, and lactotrophs, each specialized for specific secretions. 

It produces growth hormone (GH), thyroid-stimulating hormone (TSH), adrenocorticotropic hormone (ACTH), follicle-stimulating hormone (FSH), luteinizing hormone (LH), prolactin (PRL), and melanocyte-stimulating hormone (MSH). 

2. Posterior pituitary: A neural extension of the hypothalamus, containing axons and terminals of magnocellular neurons, supported by pituicytes (glial-like cells). 

It releases oxytocin and antidiuretic hormone (ADH, also called vasopressin). 

**Number 2: The hypothalamus.**

Located below the thalamus and above the pituitary, it acts as a bridge between the nervous and endocrine systems, translating neural signals into endocrine responses via multiple nuclei such as paraventricular, supraoptic, arcuate and preoptic, each with specialized neurons. 

It serves as a sensory hub, regulated by internal cues such as blood glucose and external cues such as light through osmoreceptors, chemoreceptors, and neural inputs. 

It produces gonadotropin-releasing hormone (GnRH), thyrotropin-releasing hormone (TRH), corticotropin-releasing hormone (CRH), growth hormone-releasing hormone (GHRH), and somatostatin. 

Note: Oxytocin and ADH are synthesized in magnocellular neurons of the hypothalamus and transported to the posterior pituitary for storage and release. 

**Number 3: The thyroid gland.** 

This large (weighing 20–30 grams), highly vascular, butterfly-shaped gland is located in the anterior neck, below the larynx and anterior to the trachea. 

It consists of spherical follicles lined by follicular cells (thyrocytes), with parafollicular (C) cells scattered between, derived from the neural crest. 

It is primarily controlled by the hypothalamic-pituitary-thyroid (HPT) axis through TSH from the pituitary, modulated by hypothalamic TRH. 

It produces thyroxine (T4), triiodothyronine (T3), and calcitonin. 

**Number 4: The parathyroid glands.** 

These four small glands are typically located on the posterior surface of the thyroid lobes, composed of chief cells (small, eosinophilic, responsible for secretion) and oxyphil cells (larger, function unclear, possibly reserve cells). 

Chief cells monitor blood calcium via calcium-sensing receptors (CaSR), with low levels increasing activity. Vitamin D and magnesium levels also modulate function. 

They produce parathyroid hormone (PTH). 

**Number 5: The adrenal glands.** 

These two small, triangle-shaped glands sit atop the kidneys. 

They consist of:

Their main role is to produce hormones such as cortisol, aldosterone, dehydroepiandrosterone (DHEA), and adrenaline in response to stimuli from the pituitary gland. 

**Number 6: The pineal gland.** 

This small, pinecone-shaped gland is located in the brain’s midline, near the thalamus, attached to the roof of the posterior third ventricle, lying outside the blood-brain barrier. 

It contains pinealocytes (neuroendocrine cells), interstitial glial cells, and calcified concretions (corpora arenacea, “brain sand”) that increase with age. 

Light exposure (via the retina-SCN pathway) inhibits activity, while darkness activates it. 

It produces melatonin. 

**Number 7: The pancreas.** 

This one is located in the retroperitoneum, behind the stomach, spanning the duodenum to the spleen, it has exocrine (acinar cells for digestive enzymes) and endocrine (islets of Langerhans) components. 

The endocrine part consists of 1 million islets, each 0.1–0.2 mm, containing beta, alpha, delta, and PP cells in a vascularized network. 

Its activity is modulated by glucose, amino acids, autonomic signals, and gut-derived signals such as incretins. 

It produces insulin, glucagon, somatostatin, and pancreatic polypeptide. 

**Number 8: The gonads.** 

These are paired organs: 

1. Ovaries (in females): Located in the pelvic cavity, suspended by mesovarian ligaments near the fallopian tubes, containing cortical follicles (oocytes surrounded by granulosa and theca cells) and a vascular medulla.

**Now let’s talk about hormone receptors.** 

First and foremost, receptors are specialized proteins typically located on the cell surface, inside the cell or in organelles that are critical for cellular communication but also overall cellular homeostasis. 

When it comes to their locations, the ones on the cell surface are used for hydrophilic ligands that cannot cross the membrane, the ones inside the cell (nuclear receptors) are used for lipophilic ligands and the ones in organelles (mitochondrial receptors) are used for specialized functions. 

The main thing to understand at this stage is that they accomplish this by binding to specific signaling molecules. 

These can be endogenous (think hormones or cytokines) but also exogenous ( think toxins).

● Hormones ● Neurotransmitters ● Growth factors 

They act as molecular sensors, where the ligand binds a specific receptor site which then induces a receptor change such as dimerization, phosphorylation or channel opening and then depending on the type of receptor (mainly nuclear or membrane receptor) the signal translocates to the nucleus, bind DNA, and regulate transcription (nuclear receptors) or activates signaling cascades such as cAMP or ion flux. 

This leads to a wide range of responses such as gene expression, cell proliferation, cell apoptosis to ion flux or enzyme activation. 

Since receptors are almost always classified based on their location, structure, ligand type or signaling mechanism, keep in mind the following. 

Nuclear receptors are intracellular receptors that have an N-terminal domain (NTD) (involved in transcriptional activation), a DNA-binding domain (DBD) (binds specific DNA sequences), a ligand-binding domain (LBD) (binds the ligand) and a hinge region (facilitates flexibility and nuclear localization) that bind lipophilic ligands such as vitamin D, steroid hormones, vitamin A or thyroid hormones and act as transcription factors. 

So basically, the ligand diffuses into the cell and binds to the receptor. 

Then, the receptor-ligand complex translocates to the nucleus (if not already there), binds DNA, and regulates gene transcription, leading to long-term effects like growth or differentiation. 

Why do we need them? 

Because the places where hormones for example are produced and the places that are utilized are not the same. 

So nuclear receptors bridge the gap between hormone production (in the testes or thyroid) and their action in distant tissues (muscle or liver), ensuring coordinated physiological responses. 

Receptor tyrosine kinases (RTKs) are cell surface receptors/transmembrane glycoproteins that bind peptide ligands like insulin or growth factors that have an extracellular ligand-binding domain a single transmembrane helix and an intracellular tyrosine kinase domain.

**Number 1: Androgen receptors.**

The N-terminal domain (NTD) contains activation function 1 (AF1), which is involved in transcriptional activation.

**Here’s a brief summary of how androgen receptors work,** 

A molecule of an androgen hormone slips through the cell’s outer membrane since it’s fat-soluble, it finds the androgen receptor and goes to the ligand-binding domain (LBD) in the cytoplasm. 

This causes the receptor to change shape and kick off the heat shock proteins (HSPs) in order to expose parts of the receptor that were hidden. 

Once activated, it travels to the cell’s nucleus with the help of nuclear localization signal (NLS) in order to influence the cell’s instructions (DNA is stored in the nucleus). 

Once in the nucleus, two activated receptors “team up” (just an analogy for dimerization) to form a “homodimer” which then binds to specific spots on the DNA called androgen response elements (AREs). 

In order to modulate gene expression, the receptor needs help from other proteins called co-activators or co-repressors that “open up” the DNA (by adding chemical tags to histones, which are like spools that DNA is wrapped around) or “close it off” to control whether genes are turned on or off. 

They also connect the receptor to the cell’s machinery that reads DNA. 

Then all these instruct the cell to make specific proteins by turning on or off certain genes, which then carry out the androgen’s effects.
