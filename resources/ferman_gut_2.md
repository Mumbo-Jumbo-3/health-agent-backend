Now a mast cell, is a type of white blood cell (bone marrow derived hemopoietic cell (it’s
derived from CD34+/CD117+ multipotent hematopoietic progenitor cells(*))) and thus
component of the immune system aka/or a multifunctional immune cell.

(*) It is derived from hematopoietic stem cells (HSCs) in the bone marrow that differentiate
into common myeloid progenitors and then they further differentiate mast cell progenitors
that "exit" the bone marrow and go to various tissues where they become mast cells, a
process that is driven signals such as stem cell factor (SCF) (a cytokine produced by stromal
cells) binding to their c-Kit receptor (CD117).

So unlike most immune cells that circulate in the blood, mast cells mature in peripheral
tissues.

They’re typically 10-15 micrometers in diameter, with a lifespan that can stretch from
weeks to months depending on the tissue environment.

Note: Instead of the term "mast cell", you might also see the terms “mastocyte” or
“labrocyte” being used.

**There are two phenotypes of human mast cells:**

1. **Mucosal mast cells (*)**

Besides histamine, these express tryptase as well (they produce and store more tryptase than
histamine), cytokines and leukotrienes.

They are mostly found in the mucosal surfaces like the respiratory and gastrointestinal tracts
(when activated by IgE mediatetd responses they contribute to allergic asthma, IBD and
food allergies).

**and**

2. **Connective tissue mast cells (**)**

These besides histamine also produce tryptase, chymase and carboxypeptidases such as
carboxypeptidase A.

**Understanding degranulation.**

First and foremost, the cytoplasm of mast cells contains 50–200 large granules / secretory
lysosomes that store a variety of inflammatory mediators such as histamine, serotonin,
proteases and cytokines in an inactive form until the cell is activated (they also contain
lysosomal proteins such as acid hydrolases).

This is possible partly because these organelles are enclosed by a membrane, which helps
maintain an environment different from the cytosol, allowing for the storage of potentially
harmful substances in an inactive form.

Their composition can vary depending on the tissue location and subtype of the mast cell,
the granules of MCT (Mast Cells containing only Tryptase) for example are generally
smaller and contain fewer proteases compared to the granules of MCTC (Mast Cells
containing both Tryptase and Chymase).

**The entire process can be broken down into 3 “phases” so to speak.**
The release of arachidonic acid, its conversion to PGH2 and the formation of specific
prostaglandins.

First, through a stimuli such as an allergen or cytokines for example, phospholipase A2
(PLA2) (a family of enzymes with over 30 types (the 3 main categories are cytosolic
(cPLA2), secretory (sPLA2),and calcium-independent (iPLA2)) that hydrolyze
phospholipids at the SN-2 position), is activated, it cleaves arachidonic acid from
phospholipids/from the phospholipid bilayer of cell membranes and leads to its release (into
the cytoplasm).

Then after this rate-limiting step in prostaglandin synthesis, cyclooxygenase
enzymes/prostaglandin-endoperoxide synthases such as COX-1 (cyclooxygenase-1) and
COX-2 (cyclooxygenase-2) come into play and add two oxygen molecules to the free
arachidonic acid in order to form prostaglandin G2 (PGG2) and then, by removing one
oxygen molecule, we get PGH2.

Now depending on the tissue, PGH2 is then turned into prostaglandins by synthases such
as:

● PGES, mPGES-1, mPGES-2 and cPGES when it comes to PGE2
● PGDS, lipocalin-type (L-PGDS) amd H-PGDS when it comes to PGD2
● (PGFS) and aldo-keto reductases (e.g., AKR1C3) when it comes to PGF2a
● PGIS when it comes to PGI2

(so the synthases or isomerases determine the final product (prostaglandins or
thromboxanes depending on what the body needs)).

They are short-lived (seconds to minutes) and broken down by 15-hydroxyprostaglandin
dehydrogenase (15-PGDH).

The type of prostaglandin produced depends on the cell and context—mast cells, for
instance, favor PGD2 during allergic responses.

Mast cells for instance "prefer" so to speak, PGD2 during type I allergic responses (mast
cells are a major source of PGD2).

In general, PGE synthase produces PGE2, PGF synthase produces PGF2α, PGD synthase
produces PGD2, thromboxane synthase produces TXA2, prostacyclin synthase produces
PGI2 and so on.

dysbiosis but we know for example that first and foremost, histamine intolerance is greatly
affected by the state of our gut health, that endotoxin exacerbates mast cell activation, h.

also impact colonic mast cells.
