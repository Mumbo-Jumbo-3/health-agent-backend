There are two essential fatty acids (PUFAs) within the human body, which are arachidonic acid (AA) and docosahexaenoic acid (DHA). Deficiency symptoms of AA can include dandruff, inflamed scaly skin (psoriasis, as an example), hair loss, low sex drive/infertility, low sex hormones, a blunted stress response in males, and an exaggerated stress response in females. Sounds familiar to the common modern life, huh? AA is also important to stomach health and repair, food intolerance prevention/resolution, defending against infection/immune system function, resolution of chronic inflammation, and the confidence to face your environment without anxiety. Now this is starting to sound more familiar to those in the online health community who begin to introduce more animal foods into their diet.

Linoleic acid (LA; oooh scary!) is the precursor for AA, and alpha-linolenic acid (ALA) is the precursor to DHA. Eicosapentaenoic acid (EPA) can also act a precursor to DHA, although it is not essential in mammals.

AA is primarily found in egg yolks and liver of any animal, and DHA is found primarily in seafood, shellfish, crustaceans, animal brains, while more trace amounts comparatively can be found in egg yolks, dairy, and various meats which shouldn’t be solely relied upon in most cases coming from the Standard American Diet.

Now with this background laid out for you all, the boogeyman linoleic acid is poorly converted into AA based on genetics (tends to be more rare than these following factors, but doesn’t hurt to analyze gene sequence), diabetes/insulin resistance, insufficient protein/calorie/carbohydrate intake, and insufficient riboflavin/B2, biotin/B7, pyridoxal/B6, calcium, zinc, NSAID, excessive fish oil (EPA specifically), and acetaminophen/Tylenol consumption, refined sucrose intake, and reheated industrialized vegetable/seed oils. A high intake of PUFA can also inhibit this conversion pathway both for LA into AA and ALA/EPA into DHA.

So it’s really not so much omega-6 PUFA in any amount (linoleic acid is actually found in even animal foods in various amounts) that is harmful, but excess compared to omega-3 PUFA, dietary, nutrient, and drug intake, as well as metabolic health.
