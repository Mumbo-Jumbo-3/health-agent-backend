**The system lesson 8: Understanding neurotransmitters** 

Firstly, if you think that this subject might not be important, think again. 

Millions of motor, electrical and chemical signals need to go through various parts of our bodies at any given moment. 

This is partly accomplished through membrane-to-membrane junctions. 

Neurotransmitters are molecules that are used in order to send signals from neurons to muscles and glands or between neurons. 

Or, neurotransmitters are chemicals released from neurons following an action potential ((~1 ms) electrical event / units of communication between neurons which become present when a neuron’s membrane potential reaches ~50mV (the action potential threshold)) if you want another definition. 

This is partly why they can be called the body’s chemical messengers. 

So remember, an action potential is a prerequisite in order for a neurotransmitter to be fired and also that the synapse, is the space between the neuron and the target cell. 

Let’s say now for example that we have two neurons, neuron A and neuron B. 

The junction between these two neurons (a 20–40nm gap between the presynaptic axon terminal and the postsynaptic dendrite) converts an electrical signal into a chemical signal in the form of neurotransmitter release when it reaches the presynaptic terminal and basically causes neuron A to release a chemical neurotransmitter which then will excite or inhibit neuron B from firing its own action potential.

Neurotransmitters fall into different groups or types based on the actions they have. There are: 

● Excitatory ● Inhibitory ● Modulatory 

So, a neurotransmitter can excite the neuron, inhibit a message or adjust the communication at the synapse. 

Now neuromodulators are a bit different as they are not restricted to the synaptic cleft between two neurons, and so can affect large numbers of neurons at once. 

Neuromodulators therefore regulate populations of neurons, while also operating over a slower time course than excitatory and inhibitory transmitters. 

On a last note, neurotropic factors include things such as BDNF (brain-derived neurotropic factor), GDNF (glial-derived neurotropic factor), IGF-1 (insulin-like growth factor 1), CNTF (ciliary neurotrophic factor) and NGF (nerve growth factor) and are endogenous secreted proteins that affect the development and maintenance of the CNS. 

Now without further ado, the main neurotransmitters that we will discuss here are: ● Glutamate ● GABA ● Histamine ● Serotonin ● Dopamine ● Acetylcholine ● Epinephrine (adrenaline) ● Norepinephrine (non adrenaline) 

**Number 1: Glutamate** 

Glutamate basically is the “gas” of our central nervous system (90% of synapses have it) and thus the primary excitatory transmitter. 

It also greatly affects neuroplasticity which is partly why an excess can prevent neurogenesis.

**Number 7: Serotonin** 

Serotonin (5-hydroxytryptamine, 5-HT) is a monoamine just like dopamine and noradrenaline are. 

Monoamines come from aromatic amino acids (phenylalanine, histidine, tyrosine and tryptophan) or decarboxylation (removing one carbon) and can be categorised into indoleamines that are derived from tryptophan and catecholamines that are derived from phenylalanine and tyrosine (in the brain)). 

Serotonin is an indoleamine and thus derived from tryptophan by TPH (tryptophan hydroxylase (the rate-limiting enzyme)) (type 1(TPH1) is found in the gut and produces 95% of the total serotonin inside the body and type 2 (TPH2) in the brain and acts mainly in the nervous system (TPH1 is modulated by gut stimuli and TPH2 by stress, light, and circadian rhythms)). 

It’s degraded by monoamine oxidase A (MAO-A) to 5-HIAA (excreted in urine) or reuptaken by serotonin transporters (SERT). 

It affects our sleep, mood, appetite, blood pressure, development, memory, libido and gut health. 

Serotonergic neurons can act both as excitatory and inhibitory. 

There are 18 serotonin receptors and subtypes. 

The receptors are the 5-HT1, 5-HT2, 5-HT3, 5-HT4, 5-HT5, 5-HT6, 5-HT7 and the amount of subreceptors depend on the receptor. 

The 5-HT1 for example has four and are named: 5-HT1A, 5-HT1B, 5-HT1C and 5-HT1D. 

The are involved in things such as cortisol release and activation of the adrenal axis, prolactin release, lipolysis, adipogenesis, ACTH release, increasing the density of estrogen receptors and more.
